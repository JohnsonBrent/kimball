A Better Truncated Normal Probability Distribution for Data Science?

Brent Johnson

2018-02-02

I’ve created an R package called “kimball” to impliment a trunctated normal distribution that I’ve found very helpful in my work appliations. I’m going to describe it here with a couple of examples that demonstrate its usefullness. In short, the kimball distribution perfect for reflecting judgemental input about a variable that one believes is positive and with a lower bound (i.e., can be left-truncated). But you can work with the distribution and see. Is it an improvement over others? Did it help you answer a key data science question? If so, please respond back and tell me your story.

There are well established algorithms and R packages for implimenting a truncated normal distribution, i.e., a bell-shaped density that’s truncated (or censored) on the left and/or right. One good implimentation is in the truncnorm R package. This post introduces the kimball distribution as an alternative formulation of the left-truncated normal distribution. What’s unique about the kimball distribution is that its two parameters are completely intuitive. One specifies a kimball distribution using just a mean and a maximum parameter–as in the distribution’s maximum likely value or the point beyond which its likelihood is neglible. By contrast, a normal distribution (and other implimentations of a truncated normal) is specifiied by a mean and a variance (or standard deviation) parameter. And while the mean parameter is straightforward, the variance (or standard deviation) is less so. Of course One learns in statitics 101 how to calcualte the variance. (It’s the average of squared deviations about the mean). But most students are seldom able to think about observed phenomena in such terms. Few data scientists have a good intuitive sense for squared deviations–especially when the disribution is truncated. In fact, other than when one of the neccesary paramters is a mean, interpretability isn’t a common feature for a great many probability density formulas. The parameters of most common probability distributions don’t all have an easy easy real-world, behavioral interpretation. And all else equal, I much prefer the ones that do.

Enter the kimball distribution. First articulated in 1947 by Bradford Kimball (note he didn’t name the distribution after himself. I did that.) and then revisited by Oates and Spencer (1962), the kimball distribution is entirely specified by just a mean and a maximum. (In its original form, the kimball distribution assumes a minimum of zero–something I relax in one of my later examples below, suggesting yet a third intuitive parameter for tose appliations taht need it). Like all distributions, the kimball has a variance–which one can calculate. (See Oates & Spencer(1962)’s equation #7 for this). But one doesn’t need a direct estimate of the variance in order to specify the distribution. One describes the Kimball distribution simply in terms of a mean value and a maximum value.

Kimball (1947) as well as Oates and Spencer (1962) first developed the distribution to estimate life tables. For example, the kimball density could be descriptive of a probability of a machine part’s failure as function of its age. Or it could describe the distribution of human adult lifespans. In these appllications the Kimball competes with the much more commonly used Gompertz distribution. But again, the Gompertz distribution’s two parameters, eta and b, are somewhat opaque. They’re in a complex relationship with the Gompertz’ mean and variance (or standard deviation).

The benefit of a distribution with interpretable parameters is that it lends itself to estimation via judgement or easily observed inputs. One can examine easily collecged data or judgments and use these to estimate its parameters. For example, per the simple survey data below, a mobile phone has a median expected life of between 1.5 to 2 years (in boht the US and in all countries) and a maxium life somewhere beyond 4 years. Looking a little more closely and mentally projecting the curve below further to the right, it appears that perhaps the expected maxkum life of a cell phone in the US is rohgly 6 years in the US and 7 years in all countries.

It’s easy to fit a corresponding probability density to these data using the dkimball function:

time <- c(0,.5,1,1.5,2,3,4,7)
observed <- c(NA,.06, .16, .17, .22, .19, .07, NA)
observed <- data.frame(time, observed)
#predicted <- dkimball(time,7,2) / sum(dkimball(time,7,2))
predicted <- dkimball(time,7,2)

Note that I’m not using a least squares or maxkum likelhood algorithm to estimate the parameters of the kimball distribution. I could indeed do so if I wanted to take the time. however, the parameters for the kimball distribution are already given to me in the observed statistics in the original image above. The parameters for the kimball are already handed to me in the graph above on the left. The Kimball’s parameters reflect the every day language one might use to describe such phenomena.

To extend the example above, I often use the kimball distribution to estimate or forecast the replaement volume for consumer and business durables. If one knows the sales in a given year of, say, the number of cell phones or automobiles or racks of cloud servers sold in a given year, the kimball distribution provides an estimate of the product’s future replacement sales volume. For example, here below is publically available data showing historic cell phone shipments.

http://www.worktruckonline.com/article/photos/252346/how-to-cost-effectively-manage-a-high-mileage-truck-fleet/9554.aspx https://energy.gov/sites/prod/files/2017/09/f37/fotw%23997_web.xlsx https://www.statista.com/statistics/199983/us-vehicle-sales-since-1951/ https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/TOTALSA https://www.bea.gov/national/xls/gap_hist.xlsx (see tab called “Table 6”)

If one makes the judgemental assumption that the maximum likely life of a car or light truck is two times its average age (a not unreasonable assumption per Wagner and Kamakura, 1989), one can immediately estimate a schedule of car and light truck retirements–many of which will be replaced.

In practice, I would apply the kimball distribution recursively to a forecast of first-time cell phone sales or shipments as well to complete a forecast of total cell phone shipments.

MOdifying the Kimball for non-zero lower bound

The Kimball distrribution assumes a minimum observed value of zero (x >= 0). This assumption goes back to Kimball’s original specification and application to life tables. After all, no human has ever lived a negative number of months or years! And zero is the default minimum when specifing dkimball() or pkimball(). In my implimentation, however, I allow an option to over-ride this zero assumption. A second application

For example, another task wich which I am sometimes tasked at work is to estimate the likely price bands for some product given just a few inputs. For example, a client may provide me with research (or an assumption) telling me the average price of a computer server is roughly $2,000, the minimum price for a stripped down version is $1,500 and the maximum price for a richly configered workplace server–one with lots of memory and processors–is $4,000. The client shares all this information with a desire to know the share servers sold in $500 price increments. The dkimball function solves this problem rather quickly:

Of course its entirely possible that a big player in this product example only sells servers in excess of $3,000. But without such disproving information, a single-moded distribution, covering only a positive range of $-values, and that has a left-leaning skew (which is most definately the case since the mean is closer to the minimum than it is to the minimum), the kimball distribution provides a natural choice.

There are a number of other distributions that I could fit to these same price assumptions. A weibull, gamma, lognormal, or normal distribution (many of these in a truncated form) could all be specified with parameters such that they have a shape similar to the above. However, I don’t have a complete set of observed data for all price bands. I don’t even have informatin on any single price band (unless one makes the trivial assumption that the entire prie range is one price band). This lack of data prevents me from using a parametric estimation method such as maximum likelihood to fit the parameters for these distributions. And without such data, I’m left to judge the parameters. And yet none of these distributions contain parameters that are all easily judged or interpretable. But the kimball distribution’s parameters (a mean, minimum, and maxium) are easily obtained. The very words used to describe the price band problem (expressing it in terms of a mean, min, and max) are precisely the kimball distribution’s neccesary and sufficient parameters.

The kimball package and distribution enables the data scientist to easily fit a wide range of data using easily observed and judgemental inputs. I keep the kimball close by when I need a probability density with intuitive and interpetable parameters.
